Culture

Montana's Lady Bootleggers

The women who kept the whiskey flowing when the law said stop

When Montana went dry in 1916 — two full years before the nation followed with the Eighteenth Amendment — the state's history books recorded the politics. What they largely forgot were the women who looked at the new law, shrugged, and got to work. Some of Montana's most resourceful, daring, and successful bootleggers were women. Juries wouldn't convict them, and it was illegal to search a woman. That made them invisible — and unstoppable.

Bertie Brown: The Best Moonshine in the Country

B
Bertie "Birdie" Brown
Homesteader · Distiller · Legend

Born ~1871 in Missouri. Arrived Montana Territory 1898. Homesteaded 1907 near Lewistown, Fergus County — land along Brickyard Creek. Raised chickens, planted wheat and oats. Made moonshine described as "the best and safest in the country." Died May 1933 — a gasoline explosion while tending her last batch. She never saw Prohibition end.

Bertie Brown was one of the first Black women to homestead independently in Montana Territory. She arrived in 1898 and spent nearly a decade establishing herself before filing her homestead claim in 1907 on land along Brickyard Creek near Lewistown. She farmed seriously — chickens, wheat, oats — and distilled seriously too. The rutted road to her place stayed busy with patrons who came for a drink or a pint bottle.

What made Bertie's operation extraordinary was its reputation. In a world full of dangerous, poorly made illicit spirits, hers was described as "the best and safest moonshine in the country." That wasn't marketing. In an era when badly distilled spirits could cause blindness or death, a reputation for safe product was life or death for the business. People came from miles away because they trusted Bertie's craft.

Historical note

Saint Liberty Whiskey honors Bertie's legacy with "Bertie's Bear Gulch Straight Bourbon," triple pot-distilled in Lolo, Montana — a small acknowledgment of a woman who spent her life practicing the craft they've turned into a business.

Bertie's story ends in tragedy. In May 1933 — just months before Prohibition's national repeal — a gasoline explosion while she was tending her last batch of moonshine killed her. She never saw the law change. She had spent the last seventeen years of her life breaking it, perfecting her craft, feeding her community, and never once stopping to wonder if anyone would remember her. They should.

Josephine Doody: The Bootleg Lady of Glacier Park

J
Josephine Doody
Dance-Hall Girl · Homesteader · Bootlegger

Former dance-hall girl. Husband Dan — a park ranger — died 1919. She took the homestead on the southern edge of Glacier National Park. Operated at least three stills in the wilderness north of Nyack. Delivered moonshine by boat on the Flathead River. Her best customers: Great Northern Railway workers. Signal: one train whistle toot per quart wanted. Wore large gold nugget earrings that stretched her earlobes. When she died, the nuggets disappeared.

Josephine Doody's story reads like a Western novel. A former dance-hall girl, she found herself homesteading on the southern edge of Glacier National Park after her ranger husband Dan died in 1919. With no husband and a remote property in the wilderness north of Nyack, she did what resourceful Montanans did: she adapted.

She operated at least three stills hidden in the wilderness and her distribution system was genuinely ingenious. Her best customers were the men working on the Great Northern Railway, and she delivered by boat on the Flathead River. The signal was elegant in its simplicity: railroad workers blew the train's whistle — one toot for each quart of moonshine they wanted. Josephine would load up the boat and meet them at the bank.

"Her shine was so good it would stop Great Northern Railway trains right in their tracks."

Josephine wore large gold nugget earrings that stretched her earlobes — a signature so distinctive it became part of her legend. When she died, the nuggets disappeared. Glacier Distilling Company, honoring that legacy, named their first spirit "Josephine's Moonshine" after her.

The Structural Advantage

Bertie and Josephine weren't exceptions — they were examples of a broader pattern. Montana women played vital roles in the Prohibition-era liquor trade for a simple structural reason: it was illegal to search a woman without a female officer present, and female officers were essentially nonexistent in rural Montana. Juries were reluctant to convict women. Law enforcement was systematically biased toward looking elsewhere.

Ranch wives ran stills while their husbands worked cattle. Boarding house operators served whiskey with dinner as a matter of course. Women drove delivery routes because they were less likely to be stopped. They hid bottles in baby carriages, laundry baskets, and flour barrels. They sewed coat pockets large enough to hold pint bottles. They built elaborate warning systems — a lantern in a window, a flag on a fence post — to alert their networks when agents came through.

Many were supporting families alone. The bootlegging income was the difference between survival and destitution. They took enormous risks — not just legal, but social. A man caught bootlegging was a lovable rogue. A woman caught was a scandal. Most kept no records. Their stories survive mainly in oral tradition, growing more colorful with each retelling. Which is probably exactly how they would have wanted it.

The Line to Today

Montana today has 40+ licensed craft distilleries. Women own and operate many of them. The independence and craftsmanship that drove Bertie Brown and Josephine Doody — the willingness to work with your hands, to trust your process, to refuse to let the law or the market or anyone else define what you're allowed to make — runs straight through to every distiller in Montana doing it today.

At Montucky Moonshine, we raise a glass to the ones whose names we know, and the many more whose names were deliberately lost to history. Montana's lady bootleggers weren't footnotes. They were the backbone of an underground economy, the keepers of community, and the living proof that the independent spirit has never belonged to just one gender.
