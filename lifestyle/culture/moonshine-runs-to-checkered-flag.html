From Moonshine Runs to the Checkered Flag

How bootlegger drivers built America's most popular motorsport

Every Sunday, millions of Americans watch stock cars circle oval tracks at nearly 200 miles per hour. NASCAR is a multi-billion-dollar industry with corporate sponsors, pit crews, and broadcast contracts. But its origins have nothing to do with any of that. NASCAR was born on dark mountain roads in the American South, behind the wheel of cars loaded with illegal moonshine — and one man's story ties the whole thing together.

Junior Johnson: Age 12, Behind the Wheel, Running Shine

J
Robert Glenn "Junior" Johnson Jr.
Moonshiner · NASCAR Champion · American Icon

Hauling moonshine by age 12. Invented the bootleg U-turn. Won the 1960 Daytona 500. Arrested in 1956, served 11 months in federal prison. Pardoned by President Ronald Reagan in 1986. Launched Midnight Moon in 2007. Died 2019.

Junior Johnson grew up in Wilkes County, North Carolina, where his family ran a moonshine operation as matter-of-factly as other families farmed corn — which, in a sense, is exactly what they were doing. He started hauling at 12. By the time he was in his late teens, he had developed something rare: the ability to push a heavily loaded, modified car to its absolute limits on unpaved mountain roads in the dark, while being chased.

The revenue agents had faster cars — eventually. Johnson's answer was better driving. He developed the bootleg turn — a controlled slide at speed that reversed direction without stopping — as a tactical maneuver to escape pursuit. The same technique became the foundation of NASCAR's drafting and passing strategies. The skills were identical. Only the context changed.

From Prison to Daytona

Johnson was arrested in 1956 — not running shine, but working at his family's still. He served 11 months in federal prison. He never ran moonshine again. He didn't need to. Racing had become the career, and he was the best in the country at it.

In 1960, Junior Johnson won the Daytona 500. He didn't win it because he had the fastest car — he didn't. He won it through drafting, a technique he developed by watching the aerodynamics of cars running bumper-to-bumper on mountain roads. He understood airflow the way a pilot understands wind: not from a textbook, but from thousands of miles of experience where getting it wrong meant getting caught.

"I started hauling liquor when I was about twelve years old. I thought that was just a way of life."
— Junior Johnson

In 1986, President Ronald Reagan pardoned Junior Johnson — the first presidential pardon of Reagan's second term. It was a recognition that Johnson's story was, in some essential way, the American story: a man who broke an unjust law, paid his price, built something extraordinary, and deserved to have his record cleared. Johnson went on to launch Midnight Moon moonshine in 2007, completing the circle from illicit product to legitimate brand.

Popcorn Sutton: The Last of the Old Guard

Marvin "Popcorn" Sutton was the last authentic practitioner of the old tradition. He started making moonshine at 16 in the Appalachian mountains of Tennessee and North Carolina and never stopped. He self-published a how-to memoir, Me and My Likker, that became a cult document. He made television appearances. He did interviews. He seemed, to outsiders, like a colorful character performing a tradition for entertainment.

He wasn't performing. When a federal undercover agent asked if he had any moonshine to sell, Sutton said he had 900 gallons ready. That was a federal felony. He was convicted, sentenced to 18 months in federal prison, and died by his own hand at 62 in 2009 rather than report. He never wanted to be a symbol. He just wanted to make whiskey the way he'd always made it, the way his people had always made it, without anyone telling him he couldn't.

What Montana Knows About This

Montana didn't produce a NASCAR champion. But the same dynamic that turned Junior Johnson into a racer played out along the Hi-Line and the Bootlegger Trail — men and women who developed extraordinary skills, intimate knowledge of terrain, and deep contempt for arbitrary authority, all in service of getting moonshine from where it was made to where it was wanted. Different roads, same spirit.

The moonshine-to-NASCAR story gets told as Southern history. But it's American history. The refusal to accept that the government gets to decide what you can make, drink, or drive — that thread runs from the Whiskey Rebellion through the Appalachian hollows and straight up the Hi-Line to Helena. Montucky Moonshine sits in that tradition. The craft. The independence. The refusal to wait for permission.
