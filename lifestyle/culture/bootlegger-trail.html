The Bootlegger Trail: Montana's Prohibition Underground

How geography made Montana a smuggler's paradise

Look at a map of Montana. The Canadian border runs along the top for 545 miles -- one of the longest international boundaries of any state. Below that line stretches a vast, sparsely populated landscape of prairie, mountain, and forest. In the 1920s, there were no radar stations, no motion sensors, no surveillance drones. There were barely any roads. For a bootlegger with a truck, a compass, and the nerve to drive through the night, Montana was not just an opportunity. It was an invitation.

The Geography of Defiance

Montana's bootlegging story begins and ends with geography. The state shares its northern border with the Canadian provinces of British Columbia, Alberta, and Saskatchewan. During American Prohibition, Canada maintained legal alcohol production (though individual provinces had their own complicated histories with temperance). Canadian distilleries and breweries operated openly, producing vast quantities of spirits and beer -- much of it destined, through various legal fictions, for "export" to countries other than the United States.

The reality, of course, was that enormous volumes of Canadian liquor found their way south across the border into Montana and from there into the rest of the American West. The border was simply too long, too remote, and too wild to patrol effectively. The entire U.S. Border Patrol, established in 1924 partly in response to the smuggling problem, initially consisted of just 450 officers to cover all of the nation's land borders. Montana's share of that force was laughably small.

The terrain itself was the smuggler's best friend. The Rocky Mountains along Montana's western border with Canada are a labyrinth of valleys, passes, and forest trails. A man on horseback or in a rugged vehicle could cross the border through dozens of unmarked routes, most of them invisible to anyone who did not know the country intimately. Further east, the rolling prairie offered different advantages: flat, open land where a fast vehicle could cover ground quickly, with sightlines that let a driver spot a law enforcement vehicle miles before it got close.

The Hi-Line Corridor

The most important smuggling route in Montana ran along what locals called the Hi-Line -- the Great Northern Railway corridor that stretched across the northern tier of the state, roughly parallel to the Canadian border. Towns along the Hi-Line -- Havre, Glasgow, Malta, Shelby, Cut Bank -- became critical nodes in the bootlegging network.

Havre, in particular, became a major transit point. Located about forty miles south of the Canadian border, Havre was a railroad hub where liquor smuggled across the border could be loaded onto freight cars and shipped to markets throughout the West. The town's underground tunnel system -- originally built to connect businesses along Main Street after a devastating fire in 1904 -- provided ideal hiding places for contraband. Today, those tunnels are a popular tourist attraction, marketed as "Havre Beneath the Streets," and the bootlegging history is a major part of their appeal.

The ranchers and farmers along the Hi-Line were often willing accomplices. Many of them supplemented their agricultural income by storing smuggled liquor in barns, root cellars, and outbuildings, earning a per-case fee for the service. Some became active runners themselves, using their intimate knowledge of the local roads and terrain to move product south from the border crossing points to the railroad towns. In communities where everyone knew everyone, and where the nearest federal agent might be a hundred miles away, the conspiracy of silence was nearly total.

Butte: The City That Refused to Be Dry

If the Hi-Line was the supply chain, Butte was the marketplace. Montana's legendary copper mining city had always been a wide-open town. At its peak in the early 1900s, Butte had a population of nearly 100,000, making it the largest city between Minneapolis and Seattle. Its workforce was overwhelmingly immigrant -- Irish, Cornish, Finnish, Serbian, Italian, Chinese -- and these communities had deep drinking traditions that no law was going to change.

When Montana went dry in 1918, Butte's response was immediate and emphatic: it ignored the law. The city's estimated three hundred saloons did not close so much as relocate. They moved into basements, back rooms, private clubs, and the extensive network of tunnels and underground passages that honeycombed the hill beneath the city streets. These subterranean spaces, originally created by mining operations, provided perfect speakeasy locations -- hidden, insulated, and nearly impossible for authorities to monitor.

Butte's political establishment was openly sympathetic to the bootleggers. The city's police force was notoriously reluctant to enforce prohibition. When federal agents arrived in town to conduct raids, local law enforcement would sometimes tip off the speakeasy operators in advance. On at least one occasion, a federal raid was met by a crowd of several hundred angry citizens who blocked the agents' vehicles and allowed the targeted establishment's operators to escape with their inventory.

The scale of Butte's underground drinking economy was staggering. Federal reports from the 1920s estimated that Butte consumed more illegal liquor per capita than virtually any other city in the country. The town's miners worked grueling shifts underground in dangerous conditions, and they wanted a drink when they came up. No law written in a distant capital was going to change that fundamental reality.

The Glacier Park Route

In Montana's mountainous west, a separate smuggling network operated through and around Glacier National Park. The park, established in 1910, straddled the Continental Divide and butted up against the Canadian border. Its vast wilderness, remote trails, and limited ranger force made it an ideal corridor for bootleggers.

Smugglers used pack trains -- strings of horses or mules -- to bring Canadian whiskey through mountain passes that were far from any road. Some of these routes followed ancient Native American trails; others were blazed specifically for the liquor trade. The product was cached at various points throughout the park and the surrounding national forest, then transported by vehicle to distribution points in Kalispell, Whitefish, and other western Montana towns.

The Blackfeet Reservation, which borders Glacier Park to the east, was another active corridor. The reservation's complex jurisdictional status -- tribal land subject to both federal and tribal law, with unclear enforcement authority -- created legal gray zones that smugglers exploited. Bootleggers who were arrested on reservation land sometimes walked free because no one was quite sure which court had jurisdiction over them.

The Players

Montana's bootlegging trade attracted a colorful cast of characters. There were the large-scale operators who ran quasi-corporate smuggling enterprises, complete with employees, schedules, and distribution networks. There were the lone wolves -- cowboys and ranchers who made occasional border runs to supplement their income. There were the women who ran speakeasies and hid inventory. And there were the corrupt officials who looked the other way in exchange for cash or liquor.

On the other side of the law were the "revenuers" -- federal Prohibition agents who were undermanned, underfunded, and frequently outmatched. Montana was an enormous territory to cover, and the agents were spread impossibly thin. Many were genuinely dedicated public servants who worked in dangerous conditions with little support. Others were spectacularly corrupt, accepting bribes from the very bootleggers they were supposed to be catching.

The cat-and-mouse dynamic between bootleggers and agents became the stuff of legend. Stories circulated of runners who outran pursuit cars on mountain roads, of secret compartments built into vehicles that could fool even a thorough search, of coded communication systems that would warn an entire network within hours that agents were in the area. Many of these stories grew in the telling, but the underlying reality was genuine: Montana's Prohibition era was a sustained, low-level conflict between the federal government and a population that had decided, collectively and with remarkable consistency, that the law was wrong.

Modern-Day Heritage Tourism

Today, Montana's bootlegging heritage has become a tourism asset. The underground tunnels of Havre and Butte offer guided tours that bring the Prohibition era to life. Historic bars and buildings throughout the state bear plaques and markers noting their bootlegging connections. Heritage trails connect sites associated with the smuggling trade, offering visitors a chance to drive the same routes that runners once traveled under cover of darkness.

Montana's forty-plus craft distilleries are themselves part of this heritage story. Many of them consciously reference the state's bootlegging past in their branding, their tasting room decor, and their product names. The connection is more than marketing. These distillers are the legitimate descendants of a tradition that stretches back over a century -- a tradition of Montanans making spirits with local ingredients, local knowledge, and a local attitude that has never much cared what the rest of the country thinks.

The Bootlegger Trail, as a concept and increasingly as a formalized tourism route, invites visitors to explore this history firsthand. Drive the Hi-Line from Cut Bank to Glasgow. Visit the underground tours in Havre. Walk the streets of Butte and imagine the three hundred speakeasies humming beneath your feet. Stand on the Canadian border at a remote crossing point and consider the courage -- or foolishness, depending on your perspective -- of the men and women who crossed it under darkness with a load of illegal whiskey.

At Montucky Moonshine, we carry this heritage in everything we do. Our Helena venue is built on the same ground, in the same state, that saw some of the most dramatic chapters of America's Prohibition story. The spirit of independence that drove Montana's bootleggers -- the refusal to accept someone else's rules about what you can make and enjoy -- is the same spirit that drives us today. The trail may have gone legit, but it still leads somewhere worth going.
