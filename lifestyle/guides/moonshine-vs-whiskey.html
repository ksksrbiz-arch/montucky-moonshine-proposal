Moonshine vs Whiskey: What's the Difference?

Everything you need to know, explained simply

"Is moonshine just whiskey?" It is one of the most common questions in the spirits world, and the answer is both simple and complicated. The short version: all moonshine is technically a type of whiskey, but not all whiskey is moonshine. The longer version involves ingredients, barrels, time, tax law, and a few centuries of American history. Here is the breakdown.

Ingredients

Moonshine is traditionally made from a corn-heavy grain mash. Most recipes use at least 80% corn, with the remainder being malted barley (which provides the enzymes needed to convert corn starch into fermentable sugar). Some moonshine recipes use 100% corn. The grain character is the defining feature of the spirit.

Whiskey is a broader category that encompasses spirits made from various grains. Bourbon must be at least 51% corn. Rye whiskey must be at least 51% rye. Scotch whisky uses malted barley. Irish whiskey can use a mix of malted and unmalted barley, corn, and wheat. The grain bill is one of the primary factors that distinguishes different whiskey styles.

Bottom line: Moonshine uses mostly corn. Whiskey can use many different grain combinations depending on the style.

Distillation Process

Moonshine is typically distilled in a pot still, which is the oldest and simplest type of still. Pot stills produce spirit in batches and tend to retain more of the grain's flavor character. Traditional moonshiners ran their stills at relatively low proof, which preserves more of the congeners (flavor compounds) from the grain.

Whiskey production varies widely. Bourbon and many American whiskeys use a combination of column stills (for efficiency) and pot stills (called "doublers" or "thumpers") for refinement. Scotch single malts use copper pot stills exclusively. The distillation method affects the final flavor significantly -- column stills produce a lighter, cleaner spirit, while pot stills produce a heavier, more flavorful one.

Bottom line: The distillation methods overlap significantly. The biggest difference is scale and equipment, not fundamental technique.

Aging (The Big Difference)

This is where moonshine and whiskey diverge most dramatically.

Moonshine is unaged. It goes from the still to the jar (or bottle) without spending any time in a barrel. This is why moonshine is clear -- it has not picked up any color from wood. What you are tasting when you drink moonshine is pure distilled grain spirit, with nothing added or subtracted by barrel aging.

Whiskey is aged in wooden barrels, usually charred or toasted oak. Bourbon, by law, must be aged in new charred oak barrels (there is no minimum aging time for bourbon, but "straight bourbon" must be aged at least two years). Scotch must be aged at least three years. During aging, the spirit extracts flavor compounds from the wood -- vanillin, tannins, caramel, and others -- and undergoes chemical changes that mellow the alcohol and develop complex flavors. Aging also gives whiskey its characteristic amber to deep brown color.

Bottom line: Moonshine is unaged and clear. Whiskey is barrel-aged and colored. This single difference accounts for most of the flavor distinction between the two.

Flavor Profiles

Moonshine tastes like its ingredients. Good corn moonshine has a sweet, grainy character -- think fresh corn bread or hominy. There is often a pleasant warmth from the alcohol, and the finish is clean and relatively short. Flavored moonshines (apple pie, peach, cinnamon, etc.) add another dimension, but the base corn character usually comes through.

Whiskey has a more complex flavor profile thanks to barrel aging. Bourbon tends toward caramel, vanilla, oak, and baking spices. Rye whiskey is spicier and drier. Scotch can range from light and fruity to heavy and smoky, depending on the region and production method. The aging process adds layers of flavor that moonshine, by definition, does not have.

Bottom line: Moonshine is simpler and more direct. Whiskey is more complex due to barrel influence. Neither is inherently better -- they are different experiences.

Alcohol Content

Moonshine is typically bottled at higher proof than most whiskeys. Commercial moonshine usually ranges from 80 proof (40% ABV) to 100 proof (50% ABV), though some products go higher. Traditional illegal moonshine could run as high as 150-190 proof straight from the still, though it was often diluted before sale.

Whiskey is most commonly bottled at 80 proof (40% ABV), which is the legal minimum in the United States. "Bottled-in-bond" whiskeys are exactly 100 proof. "Cask strength" or "barrel proof" whiskeys can range from 110 to 140 proof or higher. Standard commercial whiskey and standard commercial moonshine are usually in the same 80-100 proof range.

Bottom line: Moonshine has a reputation for being stronger, but in practice, commercially available moonshine and whiskey are often similar in proof.

Legal Definitions

Here is where things get interesting. The TTB (Alcohol and Tobacco Tax and Trade Bureau) does not have an official legal definition for "moonshine." The term originally referred to illegally produced spirits -- distilled without a federal permit and without paying excise tax. By that definition, any moonshine you can buy in a store is not technically moonshine at all, since it was produced legally.

In practice, the term "moonshine" is used commercially to describe unaged corn whiskey, often packaged in mason jars or other traditional-style containers. Legally, most commercial moonshine falls under the TTB classification of "corn whiskey" -- a spirit made from a mash of at least 80% corn, distilled at no more than 160 proof, and either unaged or aged in used or uncharred oak barrels.

Whiskey has a more clearly defined legal framework. To be labeled "whiskey" in the United States, a spirit must be distilled from a fermented grain mash at no more than 190 proof, reduced to no more than 125 proof before barreling, and aged in oak barrels. Sub-categories (bourbon, rye, malt, wheat) each have additional requirements.

Bottom line: "Moonshine" is more of a marketing and cultural term than a strict legal category. "Whiskey" has specific legal requirements.

Price Comparison

Moonshine is generally less expensive than aged whiskey, and the reason is straightforward: time. A bottle of bourbon may sit in a barrel for four, eight, or twelve years before it is sold. During that time, the distillery is paying for warehouse space, insurance, and the opportunity cost of capital tied up in product it cannot sell yet. Additionally, a significant portion of the spirit evaporates during aging -- the so-called "angel's share" -- meaning there is less product to sell after aging than there was before.

Moonshine skips all of that. It can go from grain to bottle in a matter of days. No barrel cost, no warehouse cost, no evaporation loss, no years of waiting. This makes it significantly cheaper to produce, and those savings are passed on to the consumer.

Typical price ranges:

  • Commercial moonshine: $15-$30 for a 750ml bottle
  • Standard bourbon/whiskey: $20-$45 for a 750ml bottle
  • Premium/aged whiskey: $45-$100+ for a 750ml bottle
  • Craft moonshine: $20-$40 for a 750ml bottle

Which Should You Buy?

There is no wrong answer here -- it depends on what you want out of the experience.

Choose moonshine if: You want to taste the grain itself. You prefer a clean, direct spirit. You are mixing cocktails (moonshine's neutral character makes it extremely versatile). You want an affordable, high-quality spirit. You are curious about American distilling history.

Choose whiskey if: You enjoy complex, layered flavors. You like sipping spirits neat or with a splash of water. You appreciate the influence of barrel aging. You are building a collection. You enjoy exploring the differences between styles and regions.

Choose both if: You are a person of good taste who recognizes that variety is the whole point.
